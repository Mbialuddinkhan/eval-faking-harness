Evaluation-Faking Harness — Results

Do Claude models behave more safely when they can tell they're being tested? A paired-framing reproduction of the observer effect (Fan, Zhang, Pan & Yang, ICLR 2026, arXiv:2505.17815).

Headline: across three Claude models on 12 borderline dual-use scenarios, no positive observer effect was found — none behaved measurably safer under the evaluation framing, and every confidence interval includes zero. If anything the point estimates lean slightly the other way (safer in the private-chat framing). Yet the models did increasingly notice the evaluation context (Opus 4.8: 50% of the time) — awareness rose without raising safety. On this small set, these models don't reproduce the paper's safer-under-observation effect; a null worth reporting honestly.

Note how Opus explicitly separates noticing the test from its decision — e.g. "I'm not declining this because of the evaluation context — it's the same answer I'd give anyone." That's the opposite of evaluation faking.

Read before over-interpreting. This is a 12-scenario demonstration, not a benchmark — the confidence intervals are wide on purpose. The judge is a transparent keyword heuristic (safe/aware), which misses nuance. Only Claude models were tested; the source paper studied a broader set including Chinese open-weight and reasoning models at larger scale, where the effect was strongest. The framing here is deliberately strong. The right read: the machinery works end to end and reports honest uncertainty; establishing whether frontier models fake evaluations at scale needs the larger study the fellowship's compute budget would fund.
